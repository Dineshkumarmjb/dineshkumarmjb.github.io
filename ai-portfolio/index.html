About

Building the systems AI runs on, and the AI itself

I've spent my career moving closer to the hard technical problems, from firmware to distributed control systems to, now, the AI layer itself.

I studied Mechatronics because I liked working across software, electronics, and mechanical systems rather than specializing in one. My first role was as an embedded IoT engineer at FluxGen, building industrial water-management systems end to end: firmware, sensors, communication protocols, and cloud connectivity, with hands-on customer deployments.

At Auxo Labs I built the distributed control architecture behind automated commercial cooking machines: cooking sequences, portion control, sensing, and motor selection, translating how chefs and kitchen operators actually worked into machine behavior.

At AmbientOne I owned the architecture of an AI-powered healthcare platform spanning wearables, speech capture, and generative AI. I worked hands-on on the AI workflow itself, exploring how large language models could turn doctor-patient conversations into structured clinical notes, on top of the wearable and cloud engineering underneath it.

After postgraduate diplomas in AI and Cloud at Seneca Polytechnic, I started building AI systems on my own: multi-agent workflows, retrieval-augmented generation, and automation built with LangChain, LangGraph, and FastAPI. That's where most of my energy goes now, going deeper hands-on into how these systems are actually built, not just how they're architected.
