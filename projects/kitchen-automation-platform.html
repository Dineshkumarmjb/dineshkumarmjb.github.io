Case study · Food-tech · Kitchen automation

On-Demand Kitchen Automation Platform

An in-house R&D program to automate the cooking itself: a family of connected machines that turn how a dish is prepared, dispensing ingredients in order and controlling the heat through each stage, into machine behavior, so a portion comes out the same way every time. I designed the machines and owned how the mechanics, motion, control, and software came together as one working system.

Role: Product Owner & Technical Lead Company: Auxo Labs Scope: Machine design, motion and control, software architecture Outcome: Working machines across two cuisines on one shared architecture
Based on a real in-house R&D program I led. Vendor specifics and exact components are abstracted. My role was product ownership, machine and control architecture, and cross-functional execution across software, hardware, design, and manufacturing, not market research or business strategy.

The problem

A commercial kitchen runs on people repeating skilled work under time pressure, and that is exactly where it strains. The same dish comes out a little different each time, portions drift, and time is lost in the rush. Before designing anything, I spent time watching how food was actually prepared, where time was wasted, and where inconsistency crept in.

The goal was to take those observations and turn them into machine behavior: a system that dispenses the right ingredients in the right order, cooks them with the right heat at each stage, and produces the same portion on every run. That meant designing not one machine but a family of them, one per dish type, and a control approach general enough to run all of them from the same base.

User workflow

The person operating the station chooses what to cook; the machine works out how. The deliberate steps are selecting the dishes and collecting the finished portions; everything between is planned and carried out by the machine.

  • Select: On the curry station, the user selects up to four dishes to cook at once, one for each of the four induction stoves.
  • Plan: The station turns the selection into a process queue, sequencing the work from the cooking time of each dish, so ingredients arrive and heat changes line up with where each dish is in its process.
  • Locate: The stoves identify themselves through RF tags and proximity sensors give the moving parts their positions, so the machine acts on what it senses rather than on a fixed assumption of where everything is.
  • Move: The central cooking station and the ingredient carousel rotate in either direction, clockwise or anticlockwise, taking the shorter way round to reach each dispenser rather than a fixed route.
  • Dispense: Spices come from a rotating carousel of containers, solids and vegetables from a dispenser to one side, and oil and sauces from a liquid dispenser at the back driven by peristaltic pumps, with load-balancing sensors in the powder and liquid stations checking how much has gone in, each added at its point in the sequence.
  • Cook: The induction heat is stepped through the process instead of held at one setting, matching the stage each dish is in.
  • Serve: Up to four dishes finish in parallel, cooked to the same steps each run.

System architecture

The curry station is built around a central cooking station ringed by four induction stoves, with the ingredient sources arranged around it: a rotating spice carousel on one side, a vegetable and solids dispenser on the other, a liquid dispenser for oil and sauces at the back, and the operator at the front. Rotation, rather than a separate arm reaching to each source, is how the machine gets to everything it needs, so one motion system serves many stations.

The curry station, top down. Four induction stoves ring a central cooking station; a rotating spice carousel, a vegetable and solids dispenser, and a liquid dispenser for oil and sauces sit around it. The station and carousel turn in whichever direction is shorter to bring each dispenser to the food, so a single motion system serves every station rather than one mechanism per source.

Underneath the mechanics, each machine is a set of subsystems that has to stay in step: the drive that rotates the station and carousel, the dispensers, and the induction heat control all have to act at the right moment, or the dish is wrong. I built this as a ROS-like architecture, independent nodes that coordinate by passing messages rather than by direct wiring. Selecting the dishes builds a process queue, and the nodes work through that queue together, sharing messages as each step comes due. The stoves identify themselves to the system through RF tags, and proximity sensors give the moving parts their positions, so the machine acts on what it senses rather than on assumptions about where things are. The same architecture runs every machine in the family, not just the curry station.

The control side. Selecting the dishes builds a process queue; from there a ROS-like set of nodes, the drive, the dispensers, and the induction heat control, works through it by passing messages rather than by direct wiring. Stoves identify themselves through RF tags, proximity sensors report position, and load-balancing sensors in the powder and liquid stations check how much has been dispensed. The same architecture is reused unchanged across every machine in both cuisine lines.

Product decisions I owned

One ROS-like control architecture for every machine

Rather than program the curry station, the roti, dosa and idli makers, and the Chinese wok and noodle machines each from scratch, I built a single ROS-like architecture, independent nodes that coordinate by passing messages, and reused it across all of them. Selecting the dishes builds a process queue that the nodes work through together, so a new machine became a variation on a proven base, differing in its mechanics and recipe steps rather than in how it was controlled and coordinated.

Trade-off: more effort designing a general node architecture up front, in exchange for every later machine starting from something that already worked instead of a blank slate.

Reserve precision for where the food depends on it

I selected the motors and drivers for the machines: stepper motors to drive the motion, the rotating station and the carousels, and servo motors to drive the dispensers. Positioning the mechanics is a repeatable, bounded move that steppers handle cheaply; how much of an ingredient is dispensed is where accuracy shows up on the plate, so that is where the closed-loop servos went. In the powder and liquid stations I added load-balancing sensors, so the amount going into each dish is measured rather than assumed.

Trade-off: steppers run without position feedback, so the motion had to be designed to stay within their limits; spending servos and load sensing only where the dose matters kept cost down without giving up portion accuracy.

Design a peristaltic pump for the liquid dispensers

The liquid dispensers use peristaltic pumps that I designed, which move oil and sauces by squeezing them along a flexible tube rather than drawing them through the pump body.

Trade-off: a peristaltic pump meters more slowly than an open valve, and the tubing is a wear part that has to be replaced on a schedule.

Move to publish and subscribe messaging after losing data

The nodes first shared their messages over a direct, point-to-point transport. Under the traffic of several of them coordinating in real time, messages went missing, and a dropped message in a cooking sequence means a skipped ingredient or a wrong heat step. I moved the machines to a lightweight publish and subscribe messaging protocol built for exactly this kind of many-to-many device communication, which made the coordination reliable.

Trade-off: an added messaging layer to run and reason about, accepted because dependable delivery between subsystems was not optional in a machine that cooks.

Plan each run around cooking time and travel

Instead of a fixed routine, the curry station plans each run from the cooking time of the selected dishes and turns the station and carousel in whichever direction reaches the next dispenser sooner, while the induction heat is stepped through the process rather than held flat. That is what lets it cook up to four dishes at once without their steps colliding at a shared dispenser.

Trade-off: more coordination logic to get right, in exchange for real parallelism across the four stoves instead of cooking one dish at a time.

Build around rotation instead of many reaching arms

The layout puts the dispensers around a central cooking station and moves the food to each source by rotating, rather than giving every station its own mechanism to reach every ingredient. One motion system does the work that several would otherwise duplicate.

Trade-off: sources are reached one position at a time rather than all at once, which is why the planning that orders and shortens that travel mattered as much as the mechanics.

Tech stack

Machines / Mechanics

Central rotating cooking station with four induction stoves, rotating spice carousel, vegetable and solids dispenser, liquid dispenser for oil and sauces

Motion

Stepper motors for station and carousel positioning, servo motors for dispensing, selected motor drivers

Sensing

RF tags for induction-stove identification, proximity sensors for position, load-balancing sensors in the powder and liquid stations

Fluid handling

Purpose-designed peristaltic pumps for metered, clean dispensing of oil and sauces

Control & communication

ROS-like node architecture, a process queue built on dish selection, a publish and subscribe messaging layer, one shared architecture across all machines

Coordination

Sequencing from each dish's cooking time, shortest-direction rotation planning, stage-based induction heat control, up to four dishes at once

Indian line

Curry station, roti maker, dosa maker, idli maker

Chinese line

Wok for fried rice, noodle maker

Outcome

The program produced working machines across two cuisines: an Indian line with the curry station and the roti, dosa and idli makers, and a Chinese line with a wok for fried rice and a noodle maker, all running on the same control architecture. The curry station cooked up to four dishes at once, dispensing and heating each along its own planned sequence, and the move to publish and subscribe messaging is what made that coordination dependable. As an in-house R&D effort, the result was a set of working machines and a reusable architecture rather than a shipped commercial product.

What I'd carry forward

  • Watch the work before automating it. The machine behavior came from watching how a real kitchen actually worked, where time was lost and where results drifted, not from a spec.
  • Design the platform, not just the product. One control architecture across every machine turned each new dish type into a variation rather than a project of its own.
  • Reliable communication is a product feature. The switch to publish and subscribe messaging was invisible to anyone watching the machine, and it was the difference between coordination that held together and one that dropped steps.
  • Spend precision where it reaches the plate. Choosing servos for dispensing and steppers for motion put accuracy and cost where each belonged.
