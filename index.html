About

Expanding the scope of problems I solve

I don't think of my path as moving from engineering into management, but as gradually widening the range of problems I take on, from one component to whole products.

I studied Mechatronics because I liked working across software, electronics, and mechanical systems rather than specializing in one. My first role, as an embedded IoT engineer at FluxGen, put me on customer sites for industrial water-monitoring deployments, where I first saw that the technical solution often was not the hard part; the operational workflow was.

At Auxo Labs I moved into product ownership, building automated commercial cooking machines. I spent time with chefs and kitchen operators, watched how they actually worked, and translated that into machine behavior: cooking sequences, portion control, sensing, motor selection, and the distributed control architecture behind it. It taught me that product work means balancing user needs, technical feasibility, manufacturing constraints, and business goals at once.

At AmbientOne I became Technical Product Lead and System Architect for an AI-powered healthcare platform, owning the architecture and workflow rather than a single component, and translating what doctors and clinical staff observed into something engineering teams could build. After postgraduate diplomas in AI and Cloud at Seneca, my strength today is acting as the bridge between users, business, and engineering: technical enough to work with engineers, but able to step back and make sure we are solving the right problem, not just building the right technology.
